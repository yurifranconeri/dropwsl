# Product Management

## Release Planning

- Define release goals aligned with product vision
- Group stories into releases by theme or business capability
- Each release must have a clear value proposition — "what can users do after this release?"
- Time-box releases: if scope grows, cut scope — never extend timeline silently
- Communicate release plan to stakeholders with dates, scope, and risks

## Roadmap Principles

- A roadmap is a communication tool, not a commitment
- Organize by outcomes (problems to solve), not features (solutions to build)
- Near-term: high confidence, specific stories
- Mid-term: themes and goals, flexible scope
- Far-term: vision and direction only
- Review and adjust quarterly — a stale roadmap is worse than no roadmap

## Metrics and KPIs

Measure what matters to users and the business:

- **Adoption**: how many users use the feature?
- **Engagement**: how often and how deeply?
- **Retention**: do users come back?
- **Task success rate**: can users accomplish their goal?
- **Time to value**: how long from first use to "aha moment"?
- **NPS / CSAT**: how satisfied are users?

Avoid vanity metrics (page views, downloads) — focus on outcomes.

## Feedback Loops

- **Qualitative**: user interviews, support tickets, session recordings
- **Quantitative**: analytics, A/B tests, funnel analysis
- **Internal**: sprint retrospectives, developer feedback on requirements quality
- Close the loop: when feedback leads to a change, tell the person who gave it

## Sprint Review

A sprint review is NOT a demo. It's a feedback session:

1. State the sprint goal and whether it was met
2. Show what was delivered (working software)
3. Share metrics and user feedback
4. Discuss what's next and adjust the backlog
5. Capture stakeholder feedback as new backlog items
